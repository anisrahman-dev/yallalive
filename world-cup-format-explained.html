How the FIFA World Cup Works: Format, Groups & Qualification

Plain-English explainer

The World Cup is huge but the rules can look confusing if you only catch it once every four years. This guide breaks down the whole tournament in plain English: how many teams play, how the group stage works, what happens in the knockouts, and how qualification actually decides who's there. We also cover the brand-new 48-team format that launches at the FIFA World Cup 2026 in the USA, Canada, and Mexico, including the new Round of 32 nobody had to think about before.

The Tournament in 30 Seconds

The FIFA World Cup is played every four years. From 2026 onwards, 48 national teams compete over roughly five weeks. The tournament is split into two phases: a group stage where teams play three matches each in mini-leagues of four, then a knockout stage where it's win-or-go-home until one team lifts the trophy. The winner is decided by a single final match. No second leg, no aggregates, no away goals. One game, 90 minutes (plus extra time and penalties if needed) and you're either the champion or you go home.

The New 48-Team Format (From 2026 Onwards)

For 24 years (1998 through 2022) the World Cup had 32 teams in 8 groups of 4, with the top 2 going through to a 16-team knockout. Clean and simple. From 2026, FIFA expanded the field to 48 teams. The new format looks like this:

Phase Teams Remaining Matches
Group Stage48 → 3272
Round of 3232 → 1616
Round of 1616 → 88
Quarter-Finals8 → 44
Semi-Finals4 → 22
3rd-Place Play-off21
Final2 → 11

That's 104 matches in total, up from 64 under the old format. The winning team plays 8 games to lift the trophy, up from 7. One extra match might not sound like much, but spread across the same 39-day window with travel between three host nations, it's a serious physical step up.

The Group Stage Explained

The 48 qualified teams are split into 12 groups of 4 teams, labelled Group A through Group L. Within each group, every team plays the other three once. That's three games per team, six games per group. Results convert to points using a standard table:

  • Win: 3 points
  • Draw: 1 point each
  • Loss: 0 points

At the end of the group stage, teams are ranked 1 to 4 in each group. The top 2 from every group automatically qualify for the Round of 32 (that's 24 teams). The 8 best third-placed teams across all groups also advance. Together those 32 teams form the knockout bracket.

Tiebreakers If Teams Are Level on Points

What happens if two or three teams finish on the same points? FIFA applies these rules in this exact order:

  1. Goal difference across all group matches (goals scored minus goals conceded).
  2. Goals scored across all group matches.
  3. Head-to-head result if only two teams are tied.
  4. Head-to-head mini-table (points, goal difference, goals scored) if three or more are tied.
  5. Fair play points (yellow and red cards count against you).
  6. Drawing of lots by FIFA (basically a coin flip). This has only happened once in tournament history (Italy v Soviet Union, 1990 group stage seeding).

How "8 Best Third-Placed" Teams Are Ranked

This is where it gets interesting. After the group stage, all 12 third-placed teams are ranked head-to-head using points, goal difference, goals scored, and fair play. The top 8 of those 12 go through to the Round of 32. The other 4 go home. Coaches now have to think strategically: finishing third isn't necessarily a disaster, but it's not safe either. Three points and a respectable goal difference is usually enough to scrape into the last 32.

The Knockout Stage Explained

Once the group stage finishes, every match is win-or-go-home. No second legs, no aggregate scores. The brackets are seeded based on how teams finished in their groups, with group winners theoretically getting easier paths.

If the Match Is Tied After 90 Minutes

Three steps, in this order:

  1. Extra time: Two 15-minute halves of additional play, no Golden Goal (that rule was scrapped in 2004). The match runs the full 30 minutes regardless of who scores.
  2. Penalty shootout: Best of 5 spot kicks per side, alternating teams. If still tied after 5, sudden death takes over (each team takes one kick, first to lead at the end of a round wins).
  3. That's it. One team advances, the other is eliminated.

The Path to the Trophy

If you've qualified directly from your group, here's what's ahead of you to win the World Cup:

  • 3 group-stage matches
  • Round of 32 (1 game)
  • Round of 16 (1 game)
  • Quarter-final (1 game)
  • Semi-final (1 game)
  • Final (1 game)

That's 8 matches across 39 days. Squads carry 26 players to manage rotation, injuries, and suspensions (any player who picks up 2 yellow cards in different matches is suspended for the next game). Manage your squad badly and a key player misses the final.

How Teams Qualify for the World Cup

With 211 FIFA member nations and only 48 slots, qualification is brutal. The world is split into 6 confederations, and each one gets a fixed number of direct qualification spots. The hosts get automatic places. The remaining slots go through 2-year qualifying campaigns within each confederation. Here's how the 48 places break down for 2026:

That's 48 in total. The 2 final places are decided by an inter-confederation play-off tournament hosted in one of the host nations a few months before the World Cup itself.

How Each Confederation Runs Qualifying

Every confederation runs its own qualifying competition with different formats. Here's how the main ones work:

  • UEFA: 54 teams split into 12 groups. Group winners qualify directly (12 places). Group runners-up plus 4 best UEFA Nations League group winners go into a play-off bracket for the final 4 slots.
  • CONMEBOL: All 10 South American nations play each other home and away in a 2-year round-robin league. Top 6 qualify, 7th place goes to the inter-confederation play-off.
  • CAF: 54 teams split into 9 groups. Each group winner qualifies. Top 4 group runners-up go into an African play-off for one final place that leads to the inter-confederation play-off.
  • AFC: Multi-round elimination. 47 teams enter, dropping to 36, then 18 in the third round (the most-watched phase). Top 2 of each group of 6 qualify (8 teams), and a play-off decides the rest.
  • CONCACAF: The 3 hosts (USA, Canada, Mexico) automatically qualify. The other 32 teams compete for 3 direct slots through multi-round qualifying.
  • OFC: 11 small Oceania nations compete for 1 direct slot. The runner-up goes to the inter-confederation play-off.

The Inter-confederation Play-off

A six-team mini tournament. Two teams are seeded directly into single-leg finals. The other four play a single-leg semi-final, then meet a seeded team in a final. Two winners qualify for the World Cup. It's brutal and decided by one match each. This is where the genuine underdog stories often come from: New Zealand, Peru, Australia, and Costa Rica have all qualified via this route in past tournaments.

Squad Rules & In-Match Regulations

A few rules worth knowing if you want to actually follow the tournament closely.

Squad Size

Each team can name 26 players in their squad (up from 23 before COVID). At least 3 must be goalkeepers. A team must submit their final 26 to FIFA 6 days before the opening game.

Substitutions

Teams get 5 substitutions per match, made across a maximum of 3 stoppages. In knockout matches that go to extra time, teams get 1 additional sub for the 30-minute extra period. Concussion substitutions don't count toward your normal 5 (introduced in 2022).

VAR

The Video Assistant Referee system is used at every match. VAR can review and correct decisions on goals, penalties, red cards, and cases of mistaken identity. The on-field referee still makes the final call after watching the pitch-side monitor.

Semi-Automated Offside

First used at Qatar 2022 and continued in 2026. Cameras track 29 data points on every player, plus a sensor inside the match ball. The system gives VAR officials an automated offside indication within seconds. Manual review by the referee still confirms the final decision.

Yellow Card & Suspension Rules

Two yellow cards across different matches = 1-match ban. Yellow card slates are wiped clean after the quarter-finals, so players don't miss the final because of a yellow in the group stage. Direct red cards lead to an automatic ban of at least 1 match, sometimes longer depending on the offence.

Prize Money & What's at Stake

FIFA distributes record-breaking prize pools that grow every tournament. For the 2022 World Cup the total prize fund was around $440 million. The 2026 edition is expected to top $700 million.

  • Winner: approximately $42 million (going up for 2026)
  • Runner-up: approximately $30 million
  • 3rd place: approximately $27 million
  • 4th place: approximately $25 million
  • Quarter-finalists: approximately $17 million each
  • Round of 16: approximately $13 million each
  • Group stage exit: approximately $9 million each (with the new Round of 32, this drops slightly for 2026)

On top of the prize money, every nation gets a $1.5 million preparation grant from FIFA. Plus clubs that release players to international duty get compensation for any days their players spend at the tournament (about $10,000 per player per day in 2022).

FAQs

How many teams play in the World Cup now? expand_more

From 2026 onwards, 48 teams play in the FIFA World Cup, up from 32 at Qatar 2022. The tournament expands to 104 matches across 39 days. Full breakdown on our World Cup 2026 page.

How many matches does the winning team play? expand_more

Under the new 48-team format, the World Cup winners play 8 matches: 3 group games plus 5 knockout rounds (Round of 32, Round of 16, quarter-final, semi-final, final). That's one more than the old 32-team format.

What happens if two teams are tied in a group? expand_more

Tiebreakers go in this order: goal difference, goals scored, head-to-head result (or head-to-head mini-table for 3-way ties), fair play points, then drawing of lots. Goal difference decides most of them.

Why are there 8 best third-placed teams? expand_more

With 12 groups, the top 2 from each group (24 teams) plus 8 best third-placed teams gives you exactly 32 for the Round of 32. The 8 best third-placed teams are ranked by points, goal difference, goals scored, and fair play.

Do hosts automatically qualify? expand_more

Yes. Host nations get automatic places in the World Cup. For 2026 that means USA, Canada, and Mexico all qualify directly. With 3 hosts, that's 3 of the 48 spots filled before qualifying even begins.

What is the inter-confederation play-off? expand_more

A 6-team mini tournament that decides the final 2 World Cup spots. It's hosted in one of the host nations a few months before the World Cup itself. Two teams are seeded directly into single-leg finals. The other four play a semi-final, then a final. The 2 winners go to the World Cup.

How many substitutions are allowed? expand_more

5 substitutions per match, made across a maximum of 3 stoppages plus halftime. In knockout matches going to extra time, teams get 1 extra substitute for that period. Concussion substitutions don't count against the limit.

Why was the format expanded? expand_more

FIFA wanted to grow the tournament's global reach and revenue. More teams means more nations involved, more matches to broadcast, more sponsorship value, and more countries with a real chance of qualifying. Critics worry about diluting quality, but the new Round of 32 keeps the knockout stakes high right from the start.
