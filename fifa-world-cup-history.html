FIFA World Cup History: Every Winner, Final & Record

Complete guide, 1930 to 2022

The FIFA World Cup is the oldest and biggest single-sport event on the planet. Since the very first tournament in Uruguay back in 1930, only eight nations have ever lifted the trophy. This is the complete history of every World Cup: the winners, the hosts, the finals that changed football, and the moments fans never stop arguing about. If you want to know who won in 1958 or how many trophies Brazil have actually got, the answers are all here.

The Finals That Defined World Cup History

Some finals are remembered for the football, others for the drama, a couple for the controversy. Here are the ones still talked about decades later.

1950: The Maracanazo (Uruguay 2 to 1 Brazil)

Played in front of about 200,000 fans at the Maracanã in Rio. Brazil only needed a draw to win the trophy on home soil. Uruguay scored twice in the second half, silenced the stadium, and pulled off the most shocking result in World Cup history. The match is still known by one word in South America: Maracanazo.

1958: Pelé Arrives (Brazil 5 to 2 Sweden)

A 17-year-old Pelé scored a hat-trick in the semi-final against France, then two more in the final. Brazil won their first World Cup, and football's biggest superstar had announced himself. The 5 to 2 scoreline remains the most goals scored in a World Cup final.

1966: England's Only Trophy (England 4 to 2 West Germany)

Geoff Hurst scored the only hat-trick ever recorded in a World Cup final. The third goal, off the underside of the bar, is still being argued about today. Was it over the line? Modern goal-line tech says no, but the goal stood and England got their first and only star.

1970: Total Football Crowned (Brazil 4 to 1 Italy)

Pelé, Jairzinho, Tostão, Rivellino, Gérson. Many neutrals still rate the 1970 Brazil side as the greatest team ever assembled. The final goal by Carlos Alberto, finished off a flowing 9-pass move, is arguably the most beautiful goal in tournament history. It also gave Pelé his third winner's medal, a record no other player has matched.

1986: Maradona Inevitable (Argentina 3 to 2 West Germany)

The Mexico 86 tournament is remembered for one man. Diego Maradona's "Goal of the Century" against England (after the Hand of God in the same game) was followed by another genius display in the final. He laid on the winning assist with three Germans hanging off him. Maradona didn't just win the World Cup, he carried Argentina to it.

1998: A Nation United (France 3 to 0 Brazil)

Zidane's two headers in the final delivered France's first ever World Cup, on home soil in Paris. The "black-blanc-beur" team became a symbol of multicultural France, and the manner of the 3 to 0 win over the defending champions (Ronaldo was visibly unwell before kick-off) made it a defining moment for football and the country.

2006: Zidane's Headbutt (Italy 1 to 1 Italy, 5 to 3 on pens)

A career ending in the worst possible way. Zinedine Zidane scored a Panenka penalty to open the scoring, then in extra time headbutted Marco Materazzi in the chest and walked off into retirement with a red card. Italy lifted the trophy. Whatever Materazzi said to him, Zidane never fully explained.

2014: 7 to 1 (Germany 7 to 1 Brazil, semi-final)

Not a final, but no one forgets it. Brazil were hosting, and Germany dismantled them by half time. Five goals in the first 29 minutes. A national trauma in Brazil that still gets brought up every World Cup. Germany went on to win the final against Argentina 1 to 0 with a Mario Götze extra-time goal.

2022: The Greatest Final Ever (Argentina 3 to 3 France, 4 to 2 pens)

Messi finally got his trophy. Mbappé became the second player ever to score a hat-trick in a final and finished on the losing side. The match swung wildly: Argentina 2 to 0 up, Mbappé equalising twice in 97 seconds, Messi scoring in extra time, Mbappé responding immediately, then penalties. It's now widely regarded as the best final in World Cup history. The full story is wired into why Argentina enters 2026 as the team to beat.

World Cup Hosts: Who Has Hosted, Who Hasn't

Hosting the World Cup is a 7-year commitment for a country (FIFA picks the host 6 to 7 years out). So far only 18 nations have held the trophy on their own soil, and most of them are concentrated in Europe and the Americas.

Multiple-Time Hosts

Mexico becomes the first country to host three World Cups when 2026 kicks off (after 1970 and 1986). Italy, France, Brazil, and Germany have hosted twice each.

First-Time Continents

The tournament has steadily expanded its geography. Africa hosted for the first time in 2010 (South Africa). Asia co-hosted in 2002 (South Korea and Japan). The Middle East hosted for the first time in 2022 (Qatar). Oceania has never hosted a Men's World Cup, though Australia and New Zealand co-hosted the Women's tournament in 2023.

Host Nation Advantage

Six host nations have won the World Cup on home soil: Uruguay (1930), Italy (1934), England (1966), West Germany (1974), Argentina (1978), and France (1998). The hosts have made the semi-finals nine times. Recent hosts have struggled though: Russia went out in the quarters (2018), Qatar finished bottom of their group (2022). The pressure tends to weigh as much as the home crowd lifts.

Iconic World Cup Records

Records that have stood for decades and a few that probably never will be broken.

  • Most goals (player, all-time): Miroslav Klose (Germany), 16 goals across 4 tournaments (2002 to 2014).
  • Most goals (single tournament): Just Fontaine (France), 13 goals in 1958. A mark that has stood for over 65 years.
  • Most appearances (player): Lionel Messi (Argentina), 26 matches across 5 World Cups (2006 to 2022).
  • Youngest player ever: Norman Whiteside (Northern Ireland) at 17 years 41 days, in 1982.
  • Youngest goalscorer in a final: Pelé at 17, scoring twice for Brazil in the 1958 final.
  • Oldest goalscorer: Roger Milla (Cameroon) at 42 years 39 days, in 1994.
  • Fastest goal: Hakan Şükür (Turkey) at 11 seconds, against South Korea in the 2002 third-place play-off.
  • Biggest win: Hungary 10 to 1 El Salvador, 1982.
  • Most goals in a single match: Austria 7 to 5 Switzerland, 1954 (12 goals total).
  • Highest attendance: 199,854 at the 1950 Brazil v Uruguay final at the Maracanã (later reduced as the stadium was refurbished).
  • Most yellow cards in a match: Portugal v Netherlands, 2006 ("Battle of Nuremberg"), 16 yellow and 4 red cards.

How the Tournament Format Has Changed Over Time

The World Cup hasn't always looked the way it does now. Field sizes, group stages, and even the trophy itself have changed multiple times.

  • 1930 to 1978: 13 to 16 team field. Some years had round-robin finals instead of a single championship match.
  • 1982: Expanded to 24 teams. New second group stage added before knockouts.
  • 1986: Round of 16 introduced as the first knockout phase.
  • 1998: Expanded to 32 teams in 8 groups of 4. This format ran for 24 years.
  • 2026: Expanded to 48 teams in 12 groups of 4. New Round of 32 added. Total matches jumps from 64 to 104. See the full breakdown in our World Cup format guide.

The Trophy

The original trophy was the Jules Rimet Trophy, used from 1930 to 1970. Brazil won it permanently after their third title in 1970 (FIFA had pledged this from the start), but the trophy was stolen in 1983 and never recovered. The current trophy is the FIFA World Cup Trophy, designed by Italian artist Silvio Gazzaniga, first used in 1974. It's solid 18-carat gold, weighs 6.175 kg, and stands 36.8 cm tall. Winners take home a gold-plated bronze replica.

FAQs

Which country has won the most World Cups? expand_more

Brazil has won the FIFA World Cup five times (1958, 1962, 1970, 1994, 2002), more than any other nation. They are also the only country to have played at every single World Cup since 1930.

Who won the first ever World Cup? expand_more

Uruguay won the inaugural FIFA World Cup in 1930, beating Argentina 4 to 2 in the final at the Estadio Centenario in Montevideo. Only 13 teams took part in that first tournament.

Why were there no World Cups in 1942 and 1946? expand_more

Both tournaments were cancelled because of World War II. The 1942 edition was originally awarded jointly to Nazi Germany and Brazil. After the war, FIFA resumed the tournament in 1950 in Brazil.

Who is the all-time top scorer at World Cups? expand_more

Miroslav Klose of Germany scored 16 goals across four tournaments from 2002 to 2014, overtaking Brazil's Ronaldo (15). Full top-scorer list on our all-time scorers page.

Has any nation won the World Cup back-to-back? expand_more

Yes, twice. Italy won in 1934 and 1938. Brazil won in 1958 and 1962. No nation has managed it since, and no one has ever won three in a row.

How many World Cups did Pelé win? expand_more

Three. Pelé is the only player in history to win three World Cups, lifting the trophy with Brazil in 1958, 1962, and 1970. He was 17 years old at his first triumph.

When and where is the next World Cup? expand_more

The next World Cup is FIFA World Cup 2026, running from 11 June to 19 July 2026 across the United States, Canada, and Mexico. It will be the first ever 48-team World Cup. Full schedule, fixtures, and venues in our World Cup 2026 guide.
